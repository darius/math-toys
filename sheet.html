This is a toy for exploring arithmetic on complex numbers: it's meant to afford a direct experience with them, the way piling up blocks is a direct experience of the natural numbers. It's fancier, in offering equation solving and visualization of complex functions. This is still a sketchy prototype, though. These instructions below won't try to teach you the math, either.

Click somewhere on the canvas above (or tap, if you have a touchscreen), and a new point will appear, called a. You can drag that point around, and if you like you can rename it using the Rename button below the canvas.

Tap on this point a to select it, and a red selection circle appears around it, and where 0 and 1 were there appear a plus and a times sign, for adding and multiplying. Drag from the plus sign to a, and a new point appears, called 2a. While you were dragging, the whole canvas slides along, because that's what adding a complex number does: it displaces its argument.

If you released the mouse before you brought it to an already-created point (like a or 0 or 1), then that canceled the addition. You can however subtract: if you slide from 0 to the point opposite a, then that's like adding -a.

Once you've created another point this way, such as 2a, notice that dragging a around makes 2a move with it. You can drag 2a instead and see a move.

Now to multiply: select a again, then this time drag from the times sign at 1 on the complex plane. If you drag from there to a, you'll create a2. Try dragging a now up to the point one unit above the origin, and see a2 move to -1.

Create another point b by clicking elsewhere on the canvas. Add it to a2 by selecting one of them and dragging from 0 to the other. Now with the new a2+b still selected, click on the 'Show field' button below the canvas. A new, smaller canvas should appear, showing a view of the whole function aâ†’a2+b as a vector field: each arrow on the new canvas shows a shrunken-down picture of the value of a2+b for a particular a. Now drag b around and watch how the field changes.

(This view actually curves the arrows to approximate how the function changes in the neighborhood of each input point. I think I was getting too fancy there, but I haven't yet come back to remove this feature.)

Here's a kind of fun thing to do: create some more powers of a, say a3, a4, a5. Drag a5 around the origin and back to 1. The others arrange themselves in a regular pentagon around the origin. Try dragging the last one around the circle again, and the others will change places into a star polygon. (This may be clearer if you drag a5 a little outside the circle so that it becomes a spiral.)

Division is like subtraction: drag from the multiply sign to the reciprocal of the target. (But the vector-field display doesn't yet work with division or subtraction or the constraint-solving introduced below.)

You can introduce constraints using the 'Pin/unpin point' and 'Merge points' buttons: select a point and press the Pin button. Now when you drag another point, the pinned one won't move. Press the Pin button again to unpin it.

Merging points permanently equates them. First drag the points you want to merge till they're close together; they acquire a blue halo and 'Merge points' button becomes active. Press it.

Sticking to adding and multiplying (no dividing), the functions you can make are called polynomials. If you play with it, you'll find that every nonconstant polynomial can be dragged to any target. This would not be true of 'real' numbers. Why does it always work? I don't know! This is called the fundamental theorem of algebra, and other people have proved it; I'd like to figure it out without peeking.
